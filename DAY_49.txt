## Overview
The discussion focused on a reflective speaking prompt: each person described one past decision they would change and how it affected their present and future. The main theme across the call was regret over missed opportunities in education and career planning, especially decisions made during intermediate, degree, and campus-placement years.

## Detailed Summary
### Narender’s reflection on his campus-placement decision
Narender described one major moment he would change: not pursuing other job opportunities during his B.Tech final year after receiving a TCS offer. He explained that he accepted a lower-profile support role because he was overconfident and believed he could easily secure a better job outside campus later.

He shared that his plan was to use the TCS offer as a fallback while searching for a better role with a higher salary and stronger developer profile. However, after about 2–3 months, the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted hiring, campus recruitment, and his ability to attend dream-company interviews. As a result, he had no realistic option other than joining TCS in January 2021.

Narender estimated that if he had pursued another company at that time, he might have started with a salary of around 35,000–40,000 instead of roughly 23,000–25,000. He said that this would have improved his financial situation, reduced struggles, and possibly helped him reach a much higher salary today through better hikes and experience progression.

### Nikhil’s reflection on exam performance and career direction
Nikhil said he would change the moment when he narrowly missed a key exam by just two marks. He felt he had fixed one outcome too rigidly in his mind and did not explore broader options after that setback.

He also reflected on choosing B.Tech without fully considering alternate interests such as communication or education-related paths, even though he was interested in mathematics and systems. He noted that he later saw others in software jobs with better facilities, allowances, and work-from-home flexibility, while his own job requires office attendance and feels less aligned with his interests.

Nikhil’s main regret was about making decisions too narrowly and not fully preparing himself for the better opportunities that were available earlier.

### Rahul’s reflection on choosing intermediate after 10th
Rahul said he would change his decision after 10th grade, when he joined intermediate despite having a childhood dream of studying fashion technology. He explained that financial constraints and a lack of support prevented him from taking the fashion technology course at that time.

He shared that he completed intermediate only because he had to, not because he was interested, and finished with 55%. Even so, he said the dream is still important to him and he intends to complete a fashion technology course after his degree. His message was that deferred ambitions can still be pursued later, even if the path is delayed.

### Darahasini’s reflection on post-10th school and hostel decisions
Darahasini said she would correct the decision she made after 10th, because what seemed like a small 5% difference ended up affecting her whole career path. She explained that her mother wanted her to join MPC in a private college, but she preferred a residential college because she had spent earlier years in a hostel and wanted a more enjoyable environment with friends.

She described intentionally underperforming in one of the residential college exams so her father would not send her there. However, this backfired: she ended up neither in the residential college nor with her preferred group of friends, and instead was placed in a minority hostel with poor food and uncomfortable living conditions.

She said that later, during degree studies, things improved significantly. She got a good seat in a highly regarded life sciences institute, had a good lecturer, and realized that the teacher’s earlier advice about life and reality was correct. Her reflection was that an honest effort in that exam could have led to a very different and likely better intermediate experience.

### Navakumar’s reflection on seriousness during the final years of B.Tech
Navakumar said he would change his lack of seriousness during the last two years of his B.Tech and placement preparation. He explained that he had many options and guidance at the time, but did not take advice seriously, nor did he put enough effort into placements or skill development.

He believes this created a major gap between himself and peers who had already secured jobs in reputed organizations. He noted that those peers now have more salary, more experience, and better progression, while he is still trying to catch up.

Navakumar also said he did not use the support of family members who were trying to guide him toward career growth. He described how he allowed his mindset to become casual after returning home, and as a result he lost momentum in learning and job preparation. Later, he mentioned that attending walk-ins and meeting other job seekers helped him realize the importance of continuous learning.

He concluded that if he had shown seriousness earlier, joined an institute, or consistently developed communication and technical skills, he believes his job prospects would be much better now.

### Narender’s broader lesson on the cost of delay
After hearing everyone’s reflections, Narender expanded on the idea that even a single wasted year can create a much larger long-term loss. He used a hypothetical example involving earnings growth over years to show how missing one year in the beginning compounds into a much larger financial and career loss later.

His message was that people often underestimate the impact of lost time in their late teens and early twenties, but those delays become visible over the years through slower salary growth, weaker experience accumulation, and missed opportunities.

He emphasized that planning should begin in degree first year itself and that people should keep improving instead of becoming comfortable after one good year. He encouraged everyone to continuously learn, build skills, and create a reason for managers to value and promote them.

### Feedback on communication and vocabulary
Narender also advised Nikhil and Navakumar to improve communication and vocabulary. He pointed out that both of them often use the same words repeatedly and should actively collect and use new expressions.

Darahasini contributed several transition and linking words that can help make speech more natural, including phrases such as “moreover,” “basically,” “in other words,” “however,” “gradually,” and “eventually.” This part of the discussion turned into a brief language-practice exchange, where several participants tried to incorporate these words into their speaking.

### Planning for the next session
Toward the end, the group discussed changing the next topic from a general reflective prompt to a concept-based or technical topic. The main reason was that general topics were being taken lightly by some participants and were not always leading to balanced speaking time.

Darahasini said she would search for concept-based topics and post a poll message within five minutes so the group could decide on the next discussion theme. The call ended with everyone agreeing to continue with a more structured topic next time.
