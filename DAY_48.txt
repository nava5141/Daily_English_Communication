## Overview
The meeting was a roundtable discussion about recent life lessons, personal regrets, and the practical changes each speaker is making to improve their future. The main themes were humility, reciprocal help, financial discipline, respecting family guidance, avoiding laziness, building skills for jobs, and learning to say no to harmful decisions.

## Narender’s reflections
Narender shared the most detailed set of lessons from roughly the last two to two and a half years. He said he had previously assumed he understood life better than his parents, siblings, cousins, or friends, but later realized that other people often knew how to live better than he did. He described this as a major shift in mindset and said he is still learning how to live properly.

He also explained that when someone helps you, you should try to return help in a similar form. For example, if someone gave time, return time; if they helped financially, return financial help. He emphasized that the type of help should be matched as closely as possible, because replacing time with money, or vice versa, may feel unbalanced to the other person.

Another lesson he highlighted was not to repeatedly praise or remind someone of the help they gave you in front of them. He said doing so can cause the person to remember only the help they gave, rather than the help they received. He advised praising people in front of others if needed, but not constantly in front of the same person.

Narender also stressed family priority. He said that even if someone helped you in a major way, you should not place them above your parents, spouse, or children. According to him, it is a serious mistake to support someone else at the cost of one’s immediate family.

He then discussed financial caution, saying that regardless of confidence or hard work, having savings is essential. He gave a practical example of keeping at least 10,000 in cash at home and arranging access to around 1 lakh within a week in case of emergencies. He warned against assuming others will always be available to help when needed.

The most forceful point in his talk was about not neglecting the job that feeds you. He said he once ignored his own work to help others and later deeply regretted it. He noted that any job—watchman, housemaid, software, driving, accounting, or data entry—should be respected because it provides food and stability for the family. He said he has learned never to degrade or neglect his source of income again.

Narender ended by explaining how he is trying to overcome these issues. He said he now keeps emergency cash at home, avoids depending on uncertain support, and continues doing his current job even though he is not fully satisfied with it. He also said he is intentionally avoiding toxic expectations and is still learning how to live without repeated setbacks.

## Navakumar’s reflections
Navakumar focused on the transition from graduation to professional life and how his environment shaped his thinking. He said that during college he mainly worried about passing subjects and avoiding backlogs, but after graduation he began regretting that he had not focused earlier on skills needed for the software industry.

He connected this to the idea that people become like the company they keep. He agreed with the point that being around the wrong group can affect mindset, and said that after graduation he spent time at home where many people around him had a more village-centered way of thinking. He described feeling pulled between village expectations and the need to adapt to city life, IT culture, and career growth.

He said his family had invested money in his education and that he now feels responsible for making that investment worthwhile. He explained that being in Hyderabad and around supportive relatives changed his perspective by showing him the city, the IT corridors, and the work environment. This helped him understand what he was preparing for and pushed him to take job search and skill-building more seriously.

Navakumar also shared that he used to be fearful and lacked confidence in English communication, especially when he first moved to Hyderabad. At that time, he struggled to speak with people, avoided going out, and felt uncomfortable in English-speaking environments. He said he is now a different person than he was initially, but still needs to learn more.

He described how exposure to interviews, workplaces, and conversations with employees helped him understand professional behavior, team structure, customer handling, and salary systems. He said these experiences are teaching him how to navigate the IT industry, improve communication, and become less fearful.

## Nikhil’s reflections
Nikhil’s contribution centered on schooling, hostel life, and growing social awareness. He said that in his early schooling in a Telugu-medium environment, he did not know English and was afraid of it. Later, he moved to an English-medium gurukula-style environment and learned to adapt.

A major lesson for him was how to behave with others while living in a hostel. Because he lived with many friends and away from his parents from around 7th to 10th class, he learned how to survive independently, manage relationships, and adjust to different personalities. He said this period helped build his confidence and taught him social behavior.

In intermediate, he said he understood that English is important as a global language. He also learned how to behave respectfully with teachers and society. He said that before this stage he had limited exposure to social issues, but later he gained more awareness of how society works.

During degree studies in Karimnagar, he said he developed strong friendships and learned more about society, conflict, and people’s behavior. He mentioned that some people fight blindly without understanding their mistakes, and that degree life gave him practical lessons about choosing goals and understanding society.

He also spoke about financial pressure and admissions. He said that after getting a good rank in an exam and securing a seat in a main branch, he could not continue because of money issues and had to change plans. Later, through the support of his brother-in-law and others, he moved to Hyderabad, attended interviews, and eventually got a job.

Nikhil concluded that Hyderabad exposed him to a more advanced environment and improved his confidence. He said he is still learning, but the city, his workplace, and the support from family have changed his direction significantly.

## Rahul’s reflections
Rahul spoke about the contrast between his early academic performance and his life after intermediate. He said that before COVID he studied hard, but after the pandemic his motivation changed and he became less focused. In intermediate, he described a period of youthful behavior and poor discipline, which later made him regret his academic choices.

He said he took a gap year after intermediate because he had not achieved the marks he wanted, and he felt he did not have enough skills. He also explained that his village environment influenced him strongly and that some of his friends were involved in drug-related or risky behavior, which villagers blamed him for by association.

Rahul said that coming to Hyderabad changed the path of his life. If he had stayed in the village, he believes he would have taken a very different route, likely without the opportunities he has now. He credited his brother-in-law’s support for helping him move to the city and continue education.

## Darahasini’s reflections
Darahasini shared four major lessons shaped by the last five years. First, she said she regrets choosing MPC instead of following her mother’s suggestion, and now believes that sometimes respecting parents’ advice can lead to a better future. She emphasized that while making one’s own decision is sometimes right, listening to elders can also be wise.

Second, she admitted that after graduation she became very lazy and unproductive. She described spending time on her mobile phone, chatting, scrolling, and avoiding household work or serious job preparation. She said this mindset did not help her in interviews or in life, and she realized she needed to become active and proactive if she wanted a job.

Third, she warned against blindly trusting strangers or online schemes. She said she got involved in a network-marketing-style program after watching a video and even borrowed 4,500 rupees from friends. Later, she realized it was a fraud after checking the company information. She said this taught her to listen to her father, who had warned her, and not to ignore family advice so easily in the future.

Fourth, she said learning to say no is essential. She connected this lesson to situations where people can get into trouble by saying yes when they should refuse. She said saying no can protect one’s family, money, privacy, and future, and described it as a major life skill.

## Discussion on the next topic
At the end of the meeting, the group discussed a topic for the next session. They agreed to keep it related to the current theme but narrower in scope: focusing on one specific thing each person wants to fix in life rather than listing multiple issues. Narender suggested that the next discussion should stay centered on a single point, explain why it matters, what impact it has, and what should be changed. The group agreed to proceed with that structure.

## Additional takeaway
The meeting also included a brief meta-discussion about language improvement. Narender pointed out that although the group is speaking English, they still need to work on grammar and correct basic sentence structure, not just vocabulary. The group appeared to accept this as part of their ongoing communication practice.
